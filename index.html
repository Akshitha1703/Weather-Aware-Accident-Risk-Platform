Project Overview

The Weather-Aware Accident Risk Platform is a comprehensive data-driven system built using Power BI, integrating accident data from Kaggle with live weather details hosted on SharePoint. The goal is to help transportation departments and the public understand how temperature, precipitation, visibility, and wind affect accident likelihood and severity.

Data Source

Primary dataset: U.S. Accidents (Updated) from Kaggle, covering years 2016–2023.

Included Data

  • Accident_ID, Severity, Start_Time, End_Time, Distance, City, State
  • Weather features – Temperature, Humidity, Visibility, Wind Speed, Precipitation

Excluded Columns

Removed unnecessary fields like Amenity, Station, Bump, and Twilight data to focus on core weather–accident relationships.

Data Pipeline

The workflow includes data extraction, transformation, and visualization:

  1. Downloaded Kaggle dataset and cleaned it in Excel/Power Query.
  2. Split data into Accident_Data and Weather_Data sheets.
  3. Uploaded files to SharePoint for dynamic web connectivity.
  4. Connected SharePoint URLs into Power BI → Get Data → Web → Load.
  5. Established relationships using composite keys for hourly weather mapping.

Analysis Insights

Key Analytical Findings

  • Over 1 million accidents recorded with nearly 65% under “Fair” or “Clear” weather, indicating driver behavior plays a key role.
  • Visibility below 5 miles and precipitation over 0.3 inches increase accident likelihood by 20–30%.
  • States like Texas, Florida, and California have the highest total accidents; however, smaller states such as Vermont and Wyoming show higher weather-related severity percentages.
  • Peak accidents occur in December and January (winter) and on Fridays, aligning with bad weather and higher travel frequency.

Weather Conditions Linked to Higher Risk

Fog / MistHigh risk due to reduced visibility, severe accident share > 35%

Recommendations

For Clients & Stakeholders

  • Develop AI-driven road monitoring dashboards that use live weather feeds to predict high-risk zones.
  • Prioritize infrastructure upgrades in states with high weather severity index (e.g., Vermont, Washington, Wyoming).
  • Increase public information campaigns during months with poor weather visibility (November–January).
  • Implement dynamic warning systems, such as automatic road sign updates during rain, fog, or snowfall.

For Public Awareness

  • Avoid sudden braking or lane changes when visibility < 5 miles.
  • Check real-time weather apps before long drives; delay travel during fog or heavy rain.
  • Keep safe distance and reduce speed by 10–20 mph during snow or thunderstorms.
  • Regularly inspect tires and wipers before winter season; most accidents occur due to reduced traction and poor vision.
